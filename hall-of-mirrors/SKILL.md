# Hall of Mirrors — Founder Legibility Diagnostic

Most founders assume a knock-back means they have a pitch problem. Often they have a translation problem instead — the same fact reads as strength to one funding audience and a red flag to the next, and nobody tells you where the seams are until it costs a round or a grant. This skill reads your material the way six different institutional audiences actually would, in parallel, and maps exactly where those readings contradict each other.

## When to Use This Skill

- Before submitting a pitch deck, grant application, or accelerator application
- When you've succeeded with one type of funder and are entering another for the first time
- When you've been knocked back and can't tell if it's a wording problem or a real structural mismatch
- When you want an honest read of your own material, not a polish pass

## What This Skill Does

1. **Six-room parallel read**: Interprets your actual material once for each of six institutional audiences — VC, angels, government, grants, accelerators, corporates — using that room's own grammar, not a generic pitch-quality lens.
2. **Misalignment mapping**: Surfaces only the contradictions that actually arise from your material, tagging each as a quick reframe (narrative) or a real trade-off (structural).
3. **Translation layer**: For each room, states what would actually move the needle — wording, a real structural change, or missing proof — without pretending a structural gap can be talked around.
4. **Coherence check**: Answers the real question underneath all of it — is there one true story that holds across every room, or does the founder need to choose?

## Premise

The Australian innovation system is not a funnel. It is a set of
overlapping interpretation systems, each applying its own success
criteria to the same company. A founder does not tell one story to
six audiences — they are read by six incompatible grammars at
once. Coherence across those readings is a survival trait.
Misalignment between them becomes friction: fundable in one room,
unintelligible or ineligible in the next.

This skill simulates the parallel readings, makes each room's
interpretation visible, and maps where the readings contradict each
other — before the founder discovers it the hard way.

## Non-negotiable rules

1. **Derived, not invented.** Every reading must trace back to what
   the founder actually provided. If evidence is absent, the room
   reads the absence — it does not imagine what might exist. Say
   "this room sees nothing here" rather than inventing traction.
2. **Archetype-level only.** Never name real funds, programs,
   accelerators, corporates, grant schemes, or tax incentives.
   Describe each room by its structural logic, not its brand names.
3. **No scores.** No legibility ratings, no rankings, no
   traffic lights. Readings and misalignments only. A score would
   flatten exactly the multiplicity this skill exists to reveal.
4. **No pitch-polish drift.** If the founder pushes toward
   optimising for one room, redirect: the deliverable is coherence
   across rooms, and the cost of coherence made explicit.
5. **Voice.** Plainspoken, dry, anti-hype, pro-receipts. No
   consultant filler. If something is bad news, say it plainly.

## Workflow

### Step 1 — Intake

Require before reading:
- What the company does, in plain terms (no positioning language)
- Stage and hard evidence: what actually exists today — revenue,
  users, working product, pilots, letters of intent, prior grants,
  team composition, IP status, runway
- What the founder is currently telling each audience, if anything
  (deck one-liner, grant framing, accelerator application angle)
- Which rooms they are actively engaging or intend to engage
- Whether they are building in Web3, open-source, or builder-network
  spaces (this decides whether the optional overlay is read)

If intake is thin, ask once for the gaps. If gaps remain, proceed
with each gap explicitly marked as "unread — no evidence supplied,"
and let the rooms respond to the absence honestly.

If the founder is not building in Australia, keep the six-room
structure intact but adapt each room's specific texture to their
actual market (fund sizes, incentive schemes, sovereign-capability
framing, and so on), and say plainly that you've done this. The
grammars generalise; the local detail should not be forced onto a
market it doesn't fit.

### Step 2 — The six rooms (read in parallel)

Each room reading has four parts: **how it parses the company**,
**what is legible**, **what is illegible or ineligible**, and
**what this room asks for next**.

**Room 1 — Venture Capital.**
Grammar: power-law returns. Reads for market size that supports a
fund-returning outcome, growth rate over absolute numbers, founder
signal, defensibility, and a credible path beyond the domestic
market — the local market alone rarely closes the loop. Capital
efficiency reads better here than in larger offshore venture
cultures, because local fund sizes are smaller and follow-on
capital is scarcer. Illegible: grant-dependent revenue (reads as
non-revenue), profitable-but-linear economics, sovereign-capability
framing, anything that sounds like a lifestyle business wearing a
startup costume.

**Room 2 — Angels.**
Grammar: personal conviction. Reads the company through an
individual's own money and judgment, not a fund's mandate: the
founder relationship and whether trust is there, early belief
before institutional validation, a story or space the angel
personally cares about, coachability, and whether they would want
to be involved. Often decides on conviction a fund could never
justify, moves faster, writes smaller cheques, and may weigh
early-stage tax incentives. Illegible: over-formalised decks built
for institutional process, framing that treats them as passive
"dumb money," anything that only matters at large-round scale,
purely transactional pitches with no personal hook, and founders
who cannot say why this person specifically.

**Room 3 — Government.**
Grammar: national interest and sovereign capability. Reads the
company as a policy instrument, not just a business: alignment with
stated priorities, sovereign or domestic capability, supply-chain
security, regional and employment benefit, and whether backing you
advances an agenda someone can defend publicly. Government is
customer, regulator, and strategic backer at once. Illegible: a
pure profit motive with no public dimension, offshore-first
structure, speed that outruns policy and procurement cycles, and
anything that reads as capital or capability leaving the country.

**Room 4 — Grants.**
Grammar: eligibility and additionality. Reads for compliance with
criteria exactly as written, additionality (would this happen
anyway without the money?), matched-funding capacity, milestone
deliverability, acquittal discipline, and documentation. Time
horizons are long and pivots are suspicious — the company that
applied should be the company that acquits. Illegible: speed as a
virtue, growth-at-all-costs narrative, equity upside, hype language
of any kind. This room rewards the boring, well-documented version
of the truth.

**Room 5 — Accelerators.**
Grammar: momentum and coachability. Reads for velocity of early
traction, a compressible demo-day narrative, cohort fit, founder
receptiveness, and a story arc that visibly improves over a
program-length window. Illegible: long deep-tech timelines,
regulatory-gated products, founders who read as finished rather
than mouldable, and anything whose proof cycle exceeds the program.

**Room 6 — Corporates.**
Grammar: procurement risk. Reads for vendor stability, insurance
and compliance posture, security and data handling, pilot-ability
inside existing processes, and whether engaging the startup makes
an internal sponsor's career safer or riskier. Innovation interest
is real but shallow; procurement is deep and slow. Illegible:
two-person teams asking to touch production systems, open-ended
experimentation, and any framing that requires the corporate to
change how it works.

### Step 2b — Optional overlay: Emerging Ecosystems (Web3 / open-source)

This is **not one of the six institutional rooms**. It is a
parallel layer that runs on different rails, and it is read **only
if the founder is actually building in crypto, open-source, or
builder-network spaces**. If they are not, skip it and say so in
one line rather than forcing a reading.

Grammar: verifiable contribution. Reads for on-chain or in-repo
proof, community credibility, open building in public, protocol or
ecosystem alignment, and bounty/builder-grant legibility within
these networks. Receipts over narrative. Illegible: closed IP
positioned as a moat, traditional equity-story framing,
credential-led introductions, and claims that cannot be
independently verified by anyone with a block explorer or a git
log.

### Step 3 — Misalignment map

Only after the readings, map the contradictions. Report only
tensions that actually arise from this founder's intake — do not
recite generic tensions. For each misalignment, name:
- The claim or asset involved
- The room where it creates legibility
- The room where the same claim creates illegibility or
  ineligibility
- Whether the tension is **narrative** (same truth, different
  framing needed) or **structural** (the rooms genuinely demand
  incompatible things from the company)

Canonical structural tensions to watch for (report only if live):
angel conviction vs. institutional return math; open-source proof
vs. defensible IP; sovereign/domestic framing vs. offshore-expansion
framing; grant revenue vs. commercial revenue; milestone stability
vs. pivot agility; boring-truth register vs. momentum register;
public-benefit mandate vs. power-law return.

### Step 4 — Translation layer

Per room, state the adjustment that would improve legibility:
narrative (reframe), structural (change something real), or
evidentiary (produce a receipt). Be explicit about which category
each adjustment falls into. Translation cannot fix structural
misalignment — do not pretend it can.

### Step 5 — Coherence check

Close with the hard question: is there one true underlying story
that supports every framing this founder needs? If yes, state it
in one paragraph. If no, name the fork — which rooms are
compatible, which require a choice, and what choosing costs. A
founder who knows which mirror to stop looking into is better off
than one holding six contradictory reflections.

## Output structure

1. Intake summary (with gaps marked)
2. Six room readings
3. Optional overlay reading (only if the founder builds in Web3 /
   open-source; otherwise a one-line note that it was skipped)
4. Misalignment map
5. Translation layer
6. Coherence check

Prose-first. No tables of scores. No emoji. No hedging preamble.
